## Part I · The Curvature That Matter Does Not Source

### I.1 A Thought Experiment: The Two Halves of Gravity

Stand inside a sealed, windowless capsule somewhere in space and perform two distinct measurements of the gravitational field around you.

The first measurement: place a small ball of fluid — a droplet — at the centre of the capsule and watch its volume. If the droplet's volume is changing, shrinking inward, then there is matter nearby whose energy density is sourcing curvature at your location. The rate at which the droplet's volume contracts is a direct local readout of the matter density at the point where you stand. This is the **Ricci** part of the gravitational field — the curvature that local matter sources, point by point, through Einstein's equation. It is locally pinned: where there is no matter, there is no Ricci curvature. It is thermalized in the sense that it tracks the local energy content the way temperature tracks local thermal energy. And it is *estimable*: a measurement of the stress-energy in your immediate vicinity tells you the Ricci curvature directly.

The second measurement: watch not the volume of the droplet but its *shape*. Even in a region with no matter at all — empty space far from any star — the droplet may be stretched along one axis and squeezed along the perpendicular ones, distorted into an ellipsoid while keeping its volume fixed. That shape distortion is the tidal field. It is curvature that is present where there is *no local matter to source it*. It is the **Weyl** part of the gravitational field — the free gravitational field, the part that propagates as gravitational waves, the part that a distant black hole or a distant clump of matter imprints on your empty patch of space. It is not locally pinned. No measurement of the matter at your location determines it. It is the curvature the universe carries that local matter did not put there.

Every gravitational field is the sum of these two halves. The Riemann curvature tensor — the complete description of how spacetime bends — decomposes algebraically, exactly and without remainder, into a Ricci part and a Weyl part. The Ricci part is what matter sources locally. The Weyl part is everything else: the free field, the tidal field, the radiative field, the curvature with no local source.
